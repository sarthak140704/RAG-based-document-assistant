Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): A Short Primer

What is RAG?
Retrieval-Augmented Generation is a technique that improves large language model
answers by first retrieving relevant text from an external knowledge base and
then generating a response grounded in that retrieved context. Instead of relying
only on what a model memorized during training, a RAG system looks up fresh,
domain-specific documents at query time. This reduces hallucination and lets the
model cite its sources.

The core pipeline.
A typical RAG pipeline has four stages. First, ingestion: documents are loaded
and split into smaller overlapping chunks so that each piece fits comfortably in
the model's context window. Second, embedding: each chunk is converted into a
dense numerical vector using an embedding model such as sentence-transformers.
Third, retrieval: the user's question is embedded with the same model, and the
most similar chunks are found using a nearest-neighbour search over the vectors,
commonly measured with cosine similarity. Fourth, generation: the retrieved
chunks are inserted into a prompt and a language model produces the final answer.

Why chunking matters.
Chunking controls how much context each retrieved unit carries. Chunks that are
too large waste the context window and dilute relevance; chunks that are too small
lose important surrounding meaning. A common strategy is a sliding window with a
fixed size and a small overlap so that ideas spanning a boundary are not lost.

Embeddings and similarity.
An embedding maps text into a high-dimensional space where semantically similar
texts lie close together. When vectors are normalized to unit length, cosine
similarity between two vectors is simply their dot product, which makes retrieval
fast. Vector databases such as FAISS, Chroma, and Pinecone store these vectors
and provide efficient approximate nearest-neighbour search at scale.

Citations and trust.
A major advantage of RAG over a plain chatbot is traceability. Because every
answer is built from specific retrieved passages, the system can show exactly
which document and page each claim came from. This makes answers verifiable and
is especially valuable in research, legal, and enterprise settings.

Evaluating a RAG system.
RAG quality is usually measured on two axes. Retrieval quality asks whether the
right passages were fetched, often measured with hit rate or recall at k.
Generation quality asks whether the final answer is faithful to the retrieved
context and free of hallucination, often measured with grounding or faithfulness
scores. Tracking both metrics separately helps diagnose whether failures come
from retrieval or from generation.
